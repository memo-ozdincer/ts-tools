B. Saddle points, TSs, negative eigenvalues, HiSD/iHiSD
Classical TS search & eigenvalue following
	•	Schlegel’s review gives detailed accounts of Cerjan–Miller eigenvector following, partitioned RFO, growing string, NEB, and reduced potential surface methods.pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih+1
	•	Central assumptions: near a TS, the negative‑eigenvalue eigenvector approximates the reaction coordinate; eigenvalue shifting and partitioned RFO fix incorrect Hessian signatures locally.[storion] 
	•	Your result that at large displacements negative eigenvectors do not point toward the minimum is consistent: you are far from the local quadratic regime those methods rely on.
Sella
	•	Hermes, Sargsyan, Najm, Zador, “Sella, an open-source automation-friendly molecular saddle point optimizer,” JCTC 18, 750–766 (2022).
	◦	Redundant internals with automated generation, including dummy atoms for linear angles.[sciencedirect]

	◦	Can target index (0 for minimum, 1 for FOSP) by modifying the effective Hessian: small shifts of zero/near‑zero eigenvalues, restricted diagonalization, and constrained optimization for translations/rotations.nature+1

HiSD and iHiSD
	•	Su et al., “Improved high-index saddle dynamics for finding saddle points and solution landscapes,” arXiv:2502.03694 (2025). iHiSD defines a crossover dynamics from gradient flow to HiSD, with rigorous nonlocal convergence from outside the attraction region of a saddle. 	◦	They show that mixing gradient and saddle dynamics can enlarge basins of attraction for saddles.
	◦	But they assume you’re already in a region where unstable eigenspaces reflect useful directions; your almost orthogonal gradient/Hessian observations suggest you’re outside that regime, explaining why LM‑style crossovers hurt.
Practical handling of small negatives
	•	Manuals (Gaussian, ORCA, Q‑Chem) and practitioner notes agree: any negative frequency formally disqualifies a minimum, but tiny ones are usually treated as numerical artifacts or shallow, often symmetry‑related saddles.osti+3
	◦	Remedies: tighten SCF/geometry thresholds, recompute frequencies with smaller finite‑difference steps, perturb geometry along imaginary mode and reoptimize, or accept very small imaginary modes for noncritical tasks.arxiv+3
This is essentially “relaxed convergence in practice” without a clear theory—an opening your work can exploit.

C. Eckart conditions and projection
	•	Eckart conditions (standard refs, e.g. Wikipedia). Define a body‑fixed frame such that the mass‑weighted mean‑square displacement between reference and current geometry is minimized; translational and rotational DOFs are decoupled from vibrations.[rowansci] 
	•	Frequency/thermochemistry documentation (Rowan, ORCA) describe:
	◦	Mass‑weighting Cartesian Hessian.
	◦	Projecting out translational/rotational subspace (Eckart or acoustic sum rule) to obtain the vibrational Hessian.orca-manual.mpi-muelheim.mpg+2
Important for your “ghost” negatives:
	•	Projection is not numerically exact; any error in the rotation/translation subspace can “leak” into vibrational DOFs and slightly perturb small eigenvalues, sometimes flipping small positives to small negatives.arxiv+1
	•	ORCA explicitly mentions an acoustic sum rule that can be toggled to reduce noise‑induced imaginary frequencies; this is a concrete example of deliberately altering small eigenvalues for numerical robustness.vergil.chemistry.gatech+1

F. Optimization far from stationary points

	•	Cartis–Gould–Toint show that naive Newton can be arbitrarily bad globally, but regularized Newton (trust‑region, cubic) restores global convergence with optimal complexity.pubs.acs+2
	•	Recent adaptive regularized Newton‑CG work; SIAM J Optim 2023 and arXiv:2502.04799.faccts+1
	◦	Uses conjugate gradient with negative‑curvature monitoring inside a regularized model; if negative curvature is detected, switches to a direction based on λ min ⁡   \lambda_{\min}  λmin and its eigenvector instead of reverting to pure gradient.[faccts] 
	◦	Ensures global complexity and quadratic local convergence when near a solution.
Detecting transition from global to local phase
	•	In trust‑region/ARC frameworks, the “Newton phase” is implicitly detected by:
	◦	ρ ≈ 1  \rho\approx 1  ρ≈1 (predicted vs actual reduction match).
	◦	Trust radius stops shrinking and often expands to allow ~full Newton steps.psicode+1

G. Optimization on noisy / inexact functions
You are not in a classical stochastic setting, but “noisy” in the sense of:
	•	Inexact PES curvature far from stationary points.
	•	Numerical noise from SCF, grids, etc.
Relevant theory:
	•	Inexact Newton and trust‑region methods (standard Nocedal–Wright style). They tolerate approximate gradients/Hessians if errors decrease or are bounded in a relative sense; global convergence is preserved.
	•	ARC and variants: ARC allows approximate Hessians and approximate model minimization as long as the model is “sufficiently accurate” relative to actual decrease.arxiv+1
	◦	This matches your case: Hessians far away are inexact but still informative; your trust‑region acceptance criteria ensure you only accept steps where the quadratic model is reasonably predictive.
	•	Implicit filtering (Kelley; Irwin et al., “Neural Network Accelerated Implicit Filtering,” PMLR 2023).
	◦	Designed for noisy black‑box functions; combines stencil‑based local models with trust‑regions and quasi‑Newton updating.wikipedia+1
	◦	Shows how to design trust‑region rules that are robust under noise and how to blend local model‑based steps with exploratory sampling.
Your empirical observation that PIC‑ARC (a first‑order preconditioned cubic regularization method in internals) fails, whereas full‑Hessian shifted Newton works, is broadly consistent with this literature: when model error is large but structured, explicit curvature is more valuable than merely regularizing gradients.

J. Related problems: materials, biomolecules, ML landscapes
Materials structure relaxation
	•	Lenz et al., “Parametrically constrained geometry relaxations for high-throughput materials discovery,” npj Comput Mater 5, 67 (2019).
	◦	Uses parametric constraints to restrict relaxation to low‑dimensional manifolds (e.g., symmetry‑preserving distortions, phonon modes), stabilizing optimization from far‑displaced or dynamically unstable structures.[lib.ysu] 
	◦	On 359 materials, constrained relaxations reduce steps by ~30–50% on average and make metastable saddles (e.g., cubic ZrO₂) optimizable.[lib.ysu] 
	◦	Analogous to your case: large displacements create severe conditioning and multiple descent directions; constraining motion to selected collective modes acts as a powerful regularizer.
Biomolecular minimization
	•	Protein minimizations typically use first‑order methods followed by limited‑memory quasi‑Newton, often with bond/angle restraints that are gradually relaxed—a form of parameterized trust‑region in coordinate space.[docs.rowansci] 
	◦	Less directly relevant mathematically, but demonstrates that large‑displacement optimization in practice is almost always regularized by constraints or preconditioning, often in internal coordinates.
Nonconvex ML optimization
	•	Regularized Newton and second‑order methods (cubic regularization, trust‑region Newton‑CG) are seeing renewed interest in ML for nonconvex, high‑dimensional losses, because they converge faster in iterations even when Hessians are very ill‑conditioned and only approximated.nature+1
	◦	Echoes your experience that “Hessian is unreliable but still better than nothing”: approximate curvature information, appropriately regularized, can greatly outperform first‑order methods in complex landscapes.
